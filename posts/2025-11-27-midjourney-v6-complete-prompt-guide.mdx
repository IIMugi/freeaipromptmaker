## The Anatomy of a Basic V6 Prompt

Every great creation starts with a solid foundation. In Midjourney, that foundation is your prompt structure. While you can get amazing results with just a few words, what I've found works best for a professional structure looks something like this:

**`[Image Prompt(s)] [Text Prompt] [--parameter 1 value] [--parameter 2 value]`**

*   **Image Prompts:** You can drag and drop one or more images into your prompt to influence the style, composition, or subject. Midjourney will use them as a visual starting point.
*   **Text Prompt:** This is the heart of your idea. This is where you describe what you want to see. Be descriptive! I try to think about the subject, medium, environment, lighting, color, and mood.
*   **Parameters:** These are the little commands that fine-tune how the image gets made. They always start with a double hyphen (`--`).

Let's start with the big onesâ€”the parameters you'll probably use in almost every prompt.

## Mastering Style with `--stylize` (or `--s`)

For my money, the `--stylize` parameter is the most influential setting in your entire toolkit. It basically controls how much of Midjourney's own "artistic training" gets applied to your image.

I like to think of it as an "artistic opinion" slider.

*   **Low `--stylize` (e.g., `--s 50`):** Midjourney sticks very closely to your prompt, even if it ends up looking a bit less "artsy." This is fantastic for when you want a very literal interpretation of your words.
*   **High `--stylize` (e.g., `--s 750`):** Midjourney takes a lot more creative liberty. It will use its knowledge of good composition, color theory, and form to make your image look more polished, sometimes even ignoring a few of your specific details to do so.

The default value is `--s 100`, and the range goes from `0` all the way to `1000`.

### Example: Low vs. High Stylize

Let's see it in action with a simple prompt.

**Low Stylize Prompt:**
```
a simple line drawing of a cat sleeping on a book --s 0
```
**Result:** You'll get exactly what you asked forâ€”a very basic, unadorned line drawing. No frills.

**High Stylize Prompt:**
```
a simple line drawing of a cat sleeping on a book --s 1000
```
**Result:** This will be a much more beautiful and artistic illustration. The lines might be more elegant, you might see some subtle shading, and the whole composition will feel more deliberate and pleasing to the eye.

ðŸ’¡ **Pro Tip:** For photorealism, a lot of us have found that a lower `--stylize` value paired with the `--style raw` parameter (we'll get to that one!) works wonders. It lets the natural language of the prompt do most of the heavy lifting.

## Framing Your Vision: Aspect Ratios (`--ar`)

This one seems simple, but trust me, don't sleep on it. The `--ar` command sets the aspect ratioâ€”the width-to-height proportionâ€”of your final image. Getting this right is absolutely essential for good composition.

The default is a 1:1 square, but V6 is super flexible.

### Common Aspect Ratios:

*   `--ar 1:1`: The classic square. Great for profile pics and most Instagram posts.
*   `--ar 16:9`: Your standard widescreen cinematic ratio. Perfect for desktop wallpapers or YouTube thumbnails.
*   `--ar 9:16`: The vertical format for phone screens. Think Instagram Stories and TikTok.
*   `--ar 3:2`: A classic photography ratio you see all the time in both portrait and landscape shots.
*   `--ar 4:5`: A really popular vertical ratio for print and social media.

### Example: Creating a Cinematic Shot

```
cinematic film still from an epic fantasy movie, a lone knight on a cliff overlooking a stormy sea at sunrise, anamorphic lens flare, detailed armor, moody lighting --ar 16:9
```
Using `--ar 16:9` here immediately gives the image that widescreen, movie-like feeling. The same prompt at `--ar 1:1` would feel much less epic and cramped.

## Embracing the Unexpected: `--chaos` (or `--c`)

Feeling stuck in a creative rut? Let me introduce you to my best friend: the `--chaos` parameter. It controls how much variation you get in that initial 2x2 image grid. Itâ€™s like a little creative chaos monkey.

*   **Low `--chaos` (e.g., `--c 0`):** The four images will be very similar, almost like minor variations on a single idea. This is the default.
*   **High `--chaos` (e.g., `--c 50`):** The four initial images will be wildly different. I'm talking different compositions, different styles, and sometimes even different takes on the subject.

The range is from `0` to `100`. I love cranking up the chaos when I need to explore unexpected creative directions from a single prompt.

### Example: Exploring Character Concepts

```
concept art for a cyberpunk street samurai, male, neon-lit alley, intricate cybernetic implants, holding a katana --ar 2:3 --c 40
```
With `--c 40`, one image in your grid might be a tight close-up portrait, another a full-body action shot, a third could be in a completely different art style, and the fourth might have a totally unique color palette. Itâ€™s perfect for brainstorming.

## Fine-Tuning the Finish: Quality (`--q`)

The `--quality` (or `--q`) parameter decides how much time the AI spends generating your image. A higher quality setting uses more of your precious GPU minutes but doesn't always mean a "better" image in an artistic sense. In V6, the options are nice and simple.

*   `--q .25`: Renders 4x faster. I use this all the time for rapid prototyping and just testing out ideas. The results will be less detailed, of course.
*   `--q .5`: Renders 2x faster. A good middle ground for when you want quick results that are still pretty decent.
*   `--q 1`: The default and highest quality setting. This is what you use for your final renders.

(A quick note: there's no longer a `--q 2` in V6. The default of `1` is the max.)

ðŸ’¡ **Pro Tip:** My workflow is usually this: when I'm trying to lock in the perfect prompt, I use `--q .25` to iterate super fast without burning through my subscription hours. Once I've got the prompt exactly how I want it, I run it one last time with `--q 1` for the final, high-detail version.

## Advanced Techniques and Parameters

Ready to go beyond the basics? Okay, this is where V6 really starts to feel like magic. These parameters give you almost surgical control.

### `--style raw`

This is easily one of the most powerful and exciting additions in V6. Using `--style raw` significantly tones down that default "Midjourney look"â€”you know, that highly opinionated, super artistic aesthetic. The result is an image that feels much more photographic and follows your prompt more literally. Itâ€™s an absolute must if you're chasing photorealism.

#### Example: Raw Photorealism

```
photograph of a 1970s office, vintage computers, cluttered desks, warm fluorescent lighting, smoke in the air, captured on 35mm film --ar 3:2 --style raw --s 250
```
Here, `--style raw` combined with a moderate `--s` value tells Midjourney to skip its usual artistic flair and just produce a straight-up, realistic photograph based on my text.

### `--no` (Negative Prompting)

Telling Midjourney what you *want* is important, but telling it what you *don't* want can be just as powerful. The `--no` parameter is your negative prompt, telling the AI to try and exclude certain things from your image.

#### Example: Refining a Portrait

```
stunning portrait of a beautiful woman with freckles, natural light, shallow depth of field, 85mm lens --ar 4:5 --no makeup, jewelry
```
This prompt will actively try to remove makeup and jewelry, which in my experience, gives you a much more natural and authentic-looking portrait.

### `--tile`

Ever need a seamless, repeating pattern for a website background, a fabric print, or a game texture? The `--tile` parameter is your one-click solution. It generates an image that can be tiled perfectly next to itself without any visible seams. It's brilliant.

#### Example: Creating a Seamless Pattern

```
watercolor floral pattern, vintage botanicals, soft pastel colors, detailed petals and leaves --tile
```
The image you get from this can be used as a repeating background flawlessly.

### Multi-Prompting with `::`

This is a slightly more advanced technique that lets you blend multiple concepts and even give them different levels of importance. You just use a double colon `::` to separate your ideas. You can even add a number right after it to assign a weight.

#### Example: Blending Concepts

```
a fusion of a steampunk::2 cat::1
```
In this prompt, Midjourney will treat the "steampunk" concept as twice as important as the "cat" concept. The result will be a cat that is heavily influenced by steampunk aesthetics, rather than just a 50/50 mix.
